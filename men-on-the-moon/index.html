Men on the Moon

Exploring Moon Craters and their Names

The Moon has captivated human curiosity for centuries. Among the many features covering its surface, craters stand out as popular formations named after individuals who left their mark on science and exploration. This project highlights lunar craters, exploring their nomenclature, historical context, and the questions surrounding their significance.

Naming Rules

The IAU follows strict guidelines for lunar nomenclature, emphasizing international cooperation and avoiding political, military, or religious significance in names. Recommendations from national committees are considered, but the final selection rests with the IAU. The association also advocates for an equitable distribution of names across ethnic groups, countries, and genders on lunar maps.

A glance at the map reveals that 75% of the lunar craters are named after men from Europe. In contrast, only 60 craters bear female names, highlighting a significant gender disparity. Furthermore, the majority of these female-named craters lack individual identities, merely assigned generic female names. This representation reflects a eurocentric and male-dominated perspective in lunar nomenclature.

Naming lunar craters became feasible after the Apollo mission in 1969, allowing for the identification of both large and small structures. Subsequently, in the 1970s, almost all craters on the far side of the moon received names. Earlier attempts were limited by low-resolution images, with significant progress made post-Apollo.

The question arises: Is it necessary to name lunar craters after individuals? While space could represent a neutral zone, the current nomenclature mirrors a form of European colonization in space. The underrepresentation of women and problematic individuals in lunar names raises concerns about the values embedded in this tradition.
